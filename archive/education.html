Education

Coursework, certificates, and distance learning around advanced power.

The original site framed MOCAP as a way for students and working professionals around the world to study emerging electrochemical storage technologies through classroom instruction, online delivery, and cross-campus collaboration.

Delivery model

Traditional classroom learning plus two-way and recorded distance education across Missouri university partners.

Program approach

Academic training tied directly to battery and electrochemical practice.

MOCAP's education copy focused on exposing students and existing workers to emerging chemistry, power storage devices, and test-and-evaluation methods while leveraging faculty across multiple Missouri campuses.

MOCAP provides an opportunity for students around the world who want to learn the chemistry and engineering behind emerging electrochemical storage devices, while also helping an existing workforce expand its knowledge of complex battery systems.

Using faculty from four Missouri university campuses, MOCAP offers both traditional classroom experiences and distance-learning options. The legacy copy emphasizes two-way and recorded training for students and workers in remote parts of the state and beyond.

The program also described certificate and minor pathways in advanced power studies, with course rotations and catalog descriptions centered on electrochemistry, evaluation, design, and energy efficiency.

Summary of the education model

  • Traditional classroom instruction across partner campuses
  • Distance learning and recorded course delivery
  • Certificate or minor pathways in advanced power studies
  • Coursework that blends theory, testing, safety, and modeling

University of Missouri Graduate certificate

Energy Efficiency Engineering: Online Graduate Certificate

The site described this 12-hour online graduate certificate as a program for engineers pursuing careers in energy efficiency, consulting, manufacturing, design, construction, energy policy, and project management.

It highlighted six interdisciplinary courses taught by University of Missouri faculty in mechanical and aerospace engineering, chemical engineering, civil and environmental engineering, electrical and computer engineering, and architectural studies.

  • Admission was listed as case-by-case, without GRE requirements.
  • Applicants were expected to have an undergraduate engineering degree and a cumulative GPA of 3.0.
  • Required application materials included transcripts, a resume or CV, and coordinator consent.
  • Legacy course list: MAE 7355, ARCHST 7325, CHENG 7464, ECE 7480, MAE 7001, and CEE 7001.

Legacy contacts listed: Sanjeev Khanna, Department of Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering, and Kelly Ross at Mizzou Online.

MU ECE4001/7001 Elective

Test and Evaluation of Electrochemical Devices

The catalog description focuses on testing and evaluation of electrochemical cells and batteries, with special emphasis on safety, operational hazards, and responsible test practice.

  • Prerequisite listed: at least 3 college credit hours of chemistry.
  • Required text: Handbook of Batteries, third edition, plus instructor notes.
  • Suggested texts addressed battery hazards and quality and reliability methods.
  • Software and tools listed: MS Word, MATLAB, and LabVIEW.
  • Assessment categories included quizzes, exams, homework, reports, essays, and experiments.

The legacy topic list included basics of electrochemical devices, hazards and accident prevention, electrical testing, environmental testing, safety testing, data analysis, reliability methods, and case studies.

Grading scale on the original site: A 90-100, B 80-90, C 70-80, D 60-70, F below 60.

MU CH ENG 4XXX Modeling

Design and Modeling of Electrochemical Devices

This archived course description focused on the design of electrochemical cells and how cell architecture influences over-potential losses, diffusion behavior, electrical conductivity, and overall battery performance.

  • Prerequisite listed: at least 6 college credit hours of chemistry.
  • Students were expected to use MATLAB and work through computational modeling of battery behavior.
  • The course emphasized ideal electrochemical cells, over-potentials, reference-cell methods, experimental methods, and simulation of battery performance.
  • Evaluation categories included online quizzes, exams, homework, essays, and experiments using identified modules.

Legacy site credit: prepared by Galen J. Suppes, Professor.
